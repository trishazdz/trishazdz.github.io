research question

Nearly everyone is familiar with the feeling of being moved by music, and is sensitive to differences in the pace or structure of music, whether or not they realize it. Even infants have been shown to be capable of detecting differences in rhythmic patterns.

Previous research has found evidence of a beat-processing network of brain areas involved with rhythm perception. For example, two brain regions commonly associated with movement, the supplementary motor area (SMA) and the basal ganglia , were shown to be more active when listening to simple rhythms than when listening to complex rhythms. Although several brain areas have been identified as being involved with the perception of beats, it is still unknown how exactly the brain processes rhythm.

Data from this experiment will help us investigate how the brain processes rhythm. Specifically, it will help to answer the following research questions:

Hannon, E. E., & Trehub, S. E. (2005). Metrical categories in infancy and adulthood. Psychological Science, 16(1), 48-55.

Grahn, J., & Brett, M. (2007). Rhythm and beat perception in motor areas of the brain. Cognitive Neuroscience, Journal of, 19(5), 893-906.

Grahn, J. A., Henry, M. J., & McAuley, J. D. (2011). FMRI investigation of cross-modal interactions in beat perception: audition primes vision, but not vice versa. Neuroimage, 54(2), 1231-1243.

Grahn, J. A., & Rowe, J. B. (2009). Feeling the beat: premotor and striatal interactions in musicians and nonmusicians during beat perception. The Journal of Neuroscience, 29(23), 7540-7548.

artist info

Elisa Kim Fromboluti is a graduate student in the Timing, Attention, and Perception lab at Michigan State University in East Lansing, MI. Working with Dr. Devin McAuley, she studies how the brain uses and responds to timing and rhythmic cues in the environment, such as those found in speech and music.
